Introduction

Inclusive design is the practice of making products and interfaces that can be used and enjoyed by different kinds of people in different situations. The purpose of this document is to set out the core principles of inclusive interface design for the web and native applications. When employing inclusive interface design, you provide better experiences for readers, customers, players and collaborators.

The inclusive design approach to accessibility is intersectional. It appreciates that the abilities and disabilities of any one person can be multiple, intermittent, and dependent on changing circumstances. It addresses the relationships between social, environmental, physical, and technological conditions.

Inclusive design recognizes that people differ in their adaptations to any of these conditions. An inclusive product or interface therefore has to be flexible, and sometimes multifaceted. It is through flexibility that robustness is achieved. A robust interface is less likely to fail anyone, whoever they are.

The Principles

➜ Define purpose & outcomes

The purpose and intended outcomes of a feature should be defined as the basis for following other design principles, particularly equivalent experience.

Inclusive design is not concerned with ornamentation. Every design decision should be informed by the needs and goals of users, as established by research and consensus. Features without a user-driven mandate should not be included at all, and those that are should address users' needs as simply and directly as possible.

  • A list of links may have the purpose of navigation. It is important that all parties can locate and apprehend the list of links as a navigation region, through visual design and programmatic disclosure.
  • An image of an author might have the purpose of identification or for use in promotional material. Depending on the surrounding content the image could be considered decorative or content. If merely decorative, it has nothing of value to offer non-visually and can be hidden to assistive technologies.

➜ Put people first

Avoid techniques and technologies that marginalize certain groups of people, or otherwise get in the way of achieving the goal of inclusivity.

➜ Consider circumstance

People use your interface through shifting situations. Make sure circumstances do not prohibit a valuable experience.

➜ Aim for equivalence

Users may consume an interface in different ways, but the value they glean from the interface should be similar.

➜ Harness convention

Reveal functionality in familiar and expected ways to accelerate people's understanding of your interface.

➜ Be consistent

Offer the same information, functionality, and behavior in the same way throughout your interface.

➜ Support control

Ensure users are able to customize the interface where needed to improve their experience of it.

➜ Offer choice

For complex or nonstandard tasks, provide different ways to complete them. Different people are comfortable with different approaches.

➜ Prioritize important or frequent tasks

Bring features which are integral to the understanding and operation of the interface to the forefront.

➜ Keep focused

Provide context-specific information and functionality in a clear and timely fashion.

➜ Add value

Only include features that add value. Features that do not add value only diminish simplicity.
